Why it isn't really about sleep — and why that matters

Before we get to why the creams fail, it's worth understanding what's actually happening in the tissue, because it changes what you look for in a solution.

The skin under your eye is thinner than a credit card. There is almost no cushioning underneath it. When you spend evenings on screens — which you do, because your work demands it — the blue light tells your brain it is still daytime. So your body doesn't wind down the way it's supposed to. Your cortisol stays elevated later into the night than it should. And that keeps the tiny blood vessels around your eyes dilated and slightly leaky, letting fluid seep into the tissue underneath.

When you sleep flat, nothing is draining that fluid. The muscle around your eye goes still and the fluid just accumulates, all night. By morning, you have bags. And the blood pooled in those capillaries, showing through that paper-thin skin, reads as dark purple shadow.

So this isn't an aging problem. It isn't a willpower problem. It isn't even really a sleep problem — it's a fluid and circulation problem, the physical toll of running a high-demand life in an environment our biology was never designed for. Screens. Cortisol. The machine that never turns off. Your under-eyes are showing you the receipt.

Understanding this is important because it tells you what a real solution actually has to do: drain the accumulated fluid, shrink the dilated vessels, and rebuild the dermal structure that chronic cortisol exposure quietly degrades over time.
